Can you average a thermometer?

Does a “global temperature” even exist?

A recurring claim says no: averaging thermometers is physically meaningless, the argument goes, and the error bars swamp any signal. So let’s not argue. Let’s take thousands of real thermometers and build a global temperature record, one honest step at a time.

01 · The objection

The skeptic’s case, stated fairly

Every so often a (self-published) paper circulates arguing that the global mean surface temperature is not just uncertain but impossible: “physically meaningless,” a statistical fiction dressed up with a degree symbol. The arguments usually come in three flavors, and the honest thing to admit up front is that two of them contain a real grain of truth.

“You can’t average temperature.” Temperature is an intensive property. A mountaintop at 5C and a valley at 15C don’t combine into a system at 10C, so averaging them, the argument goes, is a category error.

“The choice of average is arbitrary.” There are infinitely many kinds of mean: arithmetic, geometric, harmonic, root-mean-square. Nothing in physics tells you which to use, so the answer is whatever you pick.

“The thermometers are too crude and too sparse.” Individual readings carry errors of half a degree or more; coverage has changed wildly over 150 years; whole oceans go unsampled. How could the average possibly be known to hundredths of a degree?

Here’s the thing: if you build a global temperature the naive way, the skeptics are basically right. It is nearly meaningless. The interesting part is why the real method isn’t, and you can watch the fix happen.

02 · The naive attempt

First, do the dumb thing

The obvious way to make a global temperature: take every station reporting in a given year, read the thermometer, average the numbers. Here is exactly that, the plain arithmetic mean of every GHCN station’s annual temperature, in degrees C, from 1850 to today.

This is the skeptics’ strongest point, and it’s correct. A raw average of absolute temperatures is dominated by the sampling, not the signal. When the network grew from a few hundred mostly-European stations to tens of thousands spanning deserts, airports, and Arctic outposts, the average moved for reasons that have nothing to do with global warming.

So the skeptic concludes: give up, it can’t be done. The scientist concludes something narrower and more useful: don’t average absolute temperatures. There’s a better quantity hiding in the same data.

03 · The key move

Stop measuring temperature. Measure change.

Here are five real stations in Colorado, from the plains up to the high Rockies. Their absolute temperatures are wildly different. Crested Butte, at 2,700 metres, averages near freezing, while Holly, out on the plains, is a dozen degrees warmer. As a set of thermometers to average, they look hopeless.

Now switch to anomalies. Instead of each station’s temperature, plot each station’s anomaly: how far that year sits above or below that station’s own 1961–1990 normal. Watch what happens.

This is the whole trick, and it’s almost embarrassingly simple. A thermometer is a lousy instrument for measuring the temperature of the planet, but an excellent instrument for measuring how much warmer or cooler it is here than usual. That second quantity, the anomaly, is what actually behaves itself when you average.

And crucially, it dissolves the “intensive property” objection. Nobody is claiming the mountaintop and the valley reach thermal equilibrium at some blended temperature. We’re averaging a field of departures from normal: a spatial average, the same kind of quantity as average rainfall or average elevation. It’s an index of the surface, not a claim about thermodynamics.

04 · Why sparse data still works

One thermometer speaks for a thousand kilometres

Those Colorado stations moved together, but they’re neighbors. Does the trick survive distance? 05 · Where the tiny error bars come from

Crude thermometers, precise average

“But each thermometer is only good to half a degree. How can the global number be good to hundredths?” Two things are happening, and they’re the reason the anomaly is knowable far more precisely than the absolute temperature ever will be.

One: anomalies barely scatter. Within a single region in a single year, absolute temperatures are all over the place, but the anomalies are tightly bunched.

Two: random errors cancel. Each station’s error (a slightly miscalibrated thermometer, a gust, a rounding) is roughly independent. Average many of them and the noise shrinks like one-over-the-square-root of the count. Drag the slider to average more stations and watch the uncertainty on the mean collapse.

06 · “But which average?”

The “arbitrary mean” objection, put to the test

A favorite argument: there are infinitely many kinds of average, physics doesn’t pick one, so the trend is whatever you choose. It sounds devastating. It’s also checkable, so let’s check it, two ways.

First, compute the global land anomaly four different ways: a proper area-weighted grid, a plain unweighted mean, the median, and a trimmed mean that throws out the extremes. If the choice were driving the result, these would disagree.

“Fine,” says the argument, “but you’re still using ordinary means. What about the exotic ones, like geometric, harmonic, root-mean-square?” Those aren’t even defined on anomalies (you can’t take a geometric mean of negative numbers), so this is the one place we apply them to absolute temperatures, in kelvin. To keep that fair, we freeze the station set: the 160 stations with an unbroken record since 1900, the same ones every year. 08 · The payoff

Put it together: a global temperature, built from scratch

Now assemble the pieces. Convert every station to an anomaly, drop the stations into an equal-area grid so no region gets an unfair vote, weight each grid cell by its true area, and average. That’s the entire method. Here is the result: a global land-temperature record, computed in a few seconds from raw GHCN data on a laptop.

And the absolute number? Also knowable

None of this makes the planet’s absolute temperature unknowable, just harder to pin down than the change. Filling those gaps is exactly what a reanalysis does: systems like ECMWF’s ERA5 fold the station, ship, buoy, aircraft, and satellite records into a physics-based weather model that produces a temperature everywhere on Earth, hour by hour. Average that field and you get a genuine global figure: roughly 14–15C for the near-surface air (the exact value depends on the period and the dataset).

Two honest caveats. That number is still a spatial average of a field, not a single thermodynamic temperature. It’s the same category the skeptics object to, and the same one we’ve been comfortable with all along for rainfall or elevation. And it’s known to a few tenths of a degree, not hundredths, because it leans on the model to bridge the unsampled ground. That precision gap is the whole reason warming is tracked as an anomaly: the absolute baseline is a solid ~15C, and the change stacked on top of it (the part that actually matters for the climate) is what we can measure to a hair.

09 · The corrections

But don’t the adjustments create the trend?

Everything so far used NOAA’s homogenized data, the version with corrections applied. That invites the sharpest data-processing objection there is: what if the corrections are the warming? It’s a fair challenge, and the honest way to settle it is to strip the adjustments out and look at the raw thermometer readings directly.

First, why touch the raw numbers at all? Because a 150-year station record isn’t only a story about the weather; it’s also a story about the instrument, and the instrument kept changing. Each of these leaves a step in the data that has nothing to do with climate:

Time of observation

When the reading is taken drifted

US volunteer observers shifted from afternoon to morning readings through the 20th century. Reset the min/max thermometer in the cool morning instead of the warm afternoon and you double-count cold days, a creeping cool bias.

→ suppresses the raw trend
Instrument change

Glass thermometers to electronic sensors

In the 1980s thousands of liquid-in-glass thermometers in wooden screens (CRS) were swapped for electronic MMTS probes, which read daily highs slightly cooler: a sudden step down across the network.

→ suppresses the raw trend
Station moves

Relocations and re-sitings

Stations get moved: off rooftops, out to airports, up or down in elevation. Each move is an abrupt jump in the record with no climatic meaning, in either direction.

→ step changes, either way
Urbanization

Cities growing around the sensor

A station once in a field can end up ringed by asphalt, adding a local warm bias. Homogenization detects and removes this, so the correction here works against the trend, not for it.

→ removes spurious warming

Homogenization finds these steps statistically: it compares each station against its many neighbors (which felt the same weather but not the same instrument swap) and corrects the breaks that show up in one record and not the others. No target trend is fed in; the neighbors are the reference.

So does it manufacture warming? Here is the global land record built two ways with the identical method: from the fully adjusted data, and from the raw, unadjusted readings.

Each figure is the 20-year LOWESS-smoothed level today minus the 1850–1900 average, so a single hot year (El Niño) or cold year (a big volcanic eruption) can’t tip the numbers.

The raw thermometers, with zero corrections, warm by about 1.9C since the late 1800s. Homogenization lifts that to roughly 2.2C: the corrections add about a fifth on top. Take them away entirely and the warming is still unmistakable; it was never the adjustments doing the work.

One more point worth making, because it’s the reverse of the usual suspicion: on land the corrections nudge warming up, but in the full global record the single largest adjustment is to ocean data (fixing the switch from insulated buckets to engine-intake and buoy measurements), and it pulls the long-term trend down. Across the whole planet, the net effect of all adjustments is to reduce the estimated warming, not inflate it. Homogenization isn’t a thumb on the scale; it’s what makes independent groups, using different stations and different corrections, converge on the same answer.

Takeaway

So, does a global temperature exist?

If you demand a single thermodynamic temperature for a planet that ranges from –60C to +50C, then no, and no serious scientist claims one. But that was never what the global temperature record is.

What exists, and what we just built, is a spatial average of anomalies: a well-defined index of how far the surface has drifted from its own baseline. It’s reproducible from raw data, robust to the choice of average, recoverable from a fraction of the stations, and pinned down to hundredths of a degree precisely because averaging kills the noise that dooms any single thermometer. It tracks the things warming is supposed to move (ocean heat, sea level, melting ice) because it’s measuring the same energy imbalance they are.

The global temperature isn’t a fragile statistical trick. It’s one of the most over-determined numbers in all of science, and you can compute it yourself before your coffee gets cold.

The skeptics get one thing right: you can’t just average raw thermometer readings. That’s the first thing every temperature dataset is built to avoid. The rest of the method is what this page walked through.

Method & reproducibility. Every figure is computed from GHCN-M v4 (NOAA, quality-controlled adjusted monthly means, ~28,000 land stations). Anomalies use a 1961–1990 baseline per station; the global series bins stations into 5°×5° cells and area-weights by cosine of latitude. NOAA’s homogenization is already applied to this data upstream (except in Section 9, which uses the raw version too); what this simplified reconstruction leaves out relative to the operational datasets is spatial interpolation, formal uncertainty modelling, and ocean data. The point is that the core method is simple, robust, and reproducible on a laptop. Land-only here; ocean temperatures are a rich second chapter.
